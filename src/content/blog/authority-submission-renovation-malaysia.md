Most home and office renovations in Malaysia do not need a government permit. But some do, and the gap between the two is where owners get caught. If your renovation changes the structure of the building, adds built-up area, alters fire escape routes, or touches a load-bearing element, you generally need formal authority submission and approval before any work begins. Start without it, and the liability for enforcement falls on you, the owner, not the contractor who began the work.

This guide explains when authority submission is required, which bodies are involved, and what the process costs, so you can confirm your own situation before committing to a renovation.

## What Authority Submission Actually Means

Authority submission is the formal process of getting your renovation plans approved by the relevant regulator before construction starts. It usually involves preparing drawings and specifications, lodging them with the right authority, and receiving an approved permit on record.

For straightforward cosmetic work, painting, re-tiling, new cabinetry, or replacing fittings, no submission is needed. The requirement is triggered by the type of work, not the size of your budget. A small structural change can require approval, while a large but purely cosmetic refit may not.

## When Does a Renovation Need a Permit in Malaysia?

The table below is a general guide. The exact threshold varies by local authority, so the safe step is to confirm your specific scope before work starts.

| Renovation work | Approval usually required? | Typical authority |
|---|---|---|
| Painting, re-tiling, flooring, cabinetry | No | None |
| Internal non-structural partitions | Sometimes | Local council |
| Removing or altering a load-bearing wall | Yes | Local council, with an engineer |
| Home extension or added built-up area | Yes | Local council |
| New covered area, porch, or roofed structure | Yes | Local council |
| Office fit-out changing fire escape routes | Yes | BOMBA, local council |
| Electrical or M&E upgrades (commercial) | Often | DOSH, local council |
| Any work in a strata unit (condo, apartment) | Building management consent | JMB or Management Corporation |

## The Authorities You May Deal With

**Local councils.** Your local authority approves building plans for structural work, extensions, and additions to built-up area. In Petaling Jaya, that is MBPJ, which processes building plan submissions through its [One Stop Centre](https://www.mbpj.gov.my/en/citizens/e-services/one-stop-centre-osc-information). Other councils such as MBSJ in Subang Jaya and DBKL in Kuala Lumpur run the same kind of process. Where the law requires it, plans must be submitted by a registered architect or engineer.

**BOMBA, the Fire and Rescue Department.** Renovations that affect fire safety, especially in offices and commercial units, may need BOMBA approval covering escape routes, fire-rated materials, and alarm or sprinkler systems. Many commercial premises must also hold a Fire Certificate, a requirement under the Fire Services Act 1988 that is renewed annually. BOMBA's [Fire Safety Division](https://www.bomba.gov.my/en/keselamatan-kebakaran-bahagian/) sets these standards.

**DOSH, the Department of Occupational Safety and Health.** Workplace renovations that touch electrical systems, machinery, or safety provisions can fall under DOSH requirements. This is most relevant for [office](/services/office-renovation/) and [commercial renovation](/services/commercial-renovation/) projects.

## Renovating a Condo or Apartment

Strata properties add a layer that landed homes do not have. Before any renovation in a condominium or serviced apartment, you generally need written consent from the building's Joint Management Body (JMB) or Management Corporation (MC). Most buildings have renovation guidelines covering permitted working hours, wet-area waterproofing rules, restrictions on hacking structural elements, and deposit requirements.

This is separate from, and in addition to, any local council approval if the work is structural. The Platinum Arena condominium we completed in Subang Jaya, a full one-month fit-out with built-ins, tiling, and M&E works, is an example of the coordination that strata renovations demand.

## What Happens If You Skip Approval

Proceeding without required approval is a risk that lands on the owner, not the contractor who cut the corner. The common consequences are:

- A stop-work order from the local authority, halting your renovation mid-project.
- Enforcement action and the cost of regularising or reversing unapproved work.
- Property that cannot be insured or refinanced because it lacks approved plans on record.
- Difficulty selling later, when a buyer's lawyer finds an unapproved extension.

Regularising work after the fact is possible, but it is usually more complex and more costly than submitting before work begins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When is building plan approval required for home extension works?

Extension works that involve structural changes, new rooms, covered areas, or an increase to built-up area generally require local council approval before work begins. The threshold varies by authority, so the safe step is to have your scope assessed before anything starts.

### Do office renovations require a permit in Malaysia?

Office work that changes fire escape routes, structural elements, partitioning walls, electrical systems, or the building facade generally requires approval from or notification to BOMBA, the local council, or DOSH. Cosmetic office refreshes usually do not.

### Can you manage authority submission for work already completed?

Yes. Be aware that the authority will usually issue a fine first. After that, the process of regularising completed work is far more complex and potentially more costly than submitting beforehand. The completed works are assessed, retrospective submissions prepared, and a path to regularisation advised.

### Does re-tiling or repainting need a permit?

No. Purely cosmetic work such as painting, re-tiling, flooring, and cabinetry does not require authority submission. Approval is triggered by structural, fire-safety, or built-up-area changes.
